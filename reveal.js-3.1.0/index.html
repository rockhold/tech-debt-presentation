The Technical Debt Metaphor

Origins / Purpose

  • Coined by Ward Cunningham (hear his thoughts on the metaphor)
  • Used to help him EXPLAIN the need for refactoring to his boss
  • Targeted towards business/financially-minded individuals
“A little debt speeds development so long as it is paid back promptly with a rewrite. [...] The danger occurs when the debt is not repaid. Every minute spent on not-quite-right code counts as interest on that debt. Entire engineering organizations can be brought to a stand-still under the debt load of an unconsolidated implementation [...]”
- Ward Cunningham

Identifying the Types and Impact of Technical Debt

All Debts are NOT Created Equal

<Find a funny pic to put here?>

Unintentional Debt

  • Non-strategic; unknowingly acquired
  • Result of poorly-written/error-prone code and poor design decisions
  • No short or long-term benefits
  • Limits capacity to remediate strategic debts
  • Complicates simple tasks over time and leads to defects

Unintentional Debt Mitigation

  • Grow knowledge of:
    • Domain
    • Languages / Tech in Use
    • Design Patterns / API Design Concepts
    • Best Practices
  • Leverage code reviews as learning opportunities and to limit how much unintentional debt is created

Important

  • Some things that should never be taken on as "debt":
    • Messy/poorly structured code
    • Generic variable/method names
    • Missing documentation (which would otherwise be beneficial)
    • Classes that are too large (and should be split up)


These things should never be done in the name of speed, as they will only ever slow you down

Short-Term Intentional Debt

  • Tactical / reactive acquisition
  • Result of late-stage decision(s) to get releases out on-time
  • Short-term benefit to release schedule
  • Long-term liability if not addressed soon after release
    • Leads to higher cost of ownership over time
    • Can lead to bugs (particularly in the case of missing tests)
    • Potential performance, scalability, maintainability impact

Long-Term Intentional Debt

  • Stategic / proactive acquisition
  • Result of long-term strategic decisions or assumptions
    • Ex: Delaying development for a platform that won't need to be supported for X number of years
  • Delay development expenses until a later date (or entirely, if end-of-life is approaching)

Intentional Debt Mitigation

  • Track the debt / Make it visible
  • Utilize designs that will allow code to change gracefully
  • Short-Term debt:
    • Create a plan to address it and execute sooner, rather than later
    • Prioritize features / user stories for releases to first look for items which can be scoped out (without the need to take on debt)
  • Long-Term debt:
    • Determine if a plan to address it is even necessary
  • Communicate with leadership to ensure debts are being paid down appropriately

Willingness to take on debt implies that development costs now are more expensive than they are assumed to be later. If this is NOT the case, reconsider whether to take on the debt at all.

Warning

Partial investment into remediating technical debt can be more expensive and damaging than delaying until a proper investment can be made.

Communication Tactics

...to build your overall strategy

But first... the challenges

  • Communicating technical concepts to business-oriented individuals is difficult or ineffective
    • We're often much more suited to address the debt than to explain to others why we should
  • Debt is often under the covers and may not be readily felt
  • Debt removal may be expensive and provide no tangible value for end-users

Tactic: Know Your Audience

  • Don't flood your audience with too much information
  • Respect their time
  • Phrase the problem in terms your audience will understand
    • For executives, projects managers, strategists, etc. this is often in terms of time
    • Leverage the financial terms inherent in the technical debt metaphor to paint a less technical picture of the situation

Tactic: Make it Visible

  • Before taking on debt, make the impact known and discuss a timeline to address it
    • Short-term / long-term effect to related projects (resource capacity and project estimates)
  • Document the impacts of existing debts which affect your project

Tactic: Do Your Homework

  • Fight the urge to rely on 'gut' feelings when communicating about tech debt
  • Put in the effort to determine the true impact
  • Consider providing:
    • Estimate of work effort to pay off the debt
    • Summary of historical impact on related projects
    • Estimate of future impact for already planned work
    • Estimate of future savings for not-yet-planned work

Double-Check Your Homework

If the data doesn't clearly indicate the best approach is to pay off the debt now, then consider changing the conversation focus from addressing the debt to making the debt more visible.

Tactic: Build Trust

Don't Do
Use scare tactics Use data
Use 'gut' feelings Use MORE data
Use tech terms to convolute or obscure Use business/financial terms
Provide unrealistic estimates in the face of debt Be realistic
Destroy trust with your product stakeholders and executives Be honest about what you do and don't know

In Summary...

Leverage the metaphor

Understand your debt

Make the debt visible

Tailor discussions for your audience

...and most of all...

Do your homework

The effectiveness of your attempts to reduce your debt depend on it.
